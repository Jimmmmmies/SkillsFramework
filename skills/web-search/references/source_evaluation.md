# Source Evaluation and Fact-Checking Guide

## CRAAP Test Framework

The CRAAP test is a framework for evaluating sources based on five criteria:

### 1. Currency: Timeliness of the Information
- When was the information published or posted?
- Has the information been revised or updated?
- Does your topic require current information, or will older sources work?
- Are the links functional?

**Questions to Ask:**
- When was this published/posted?
- Is this information current enough for my needs?
- When was it last updated?
- Are there more recent versions or editions?

### 2. Relevance: Importance to Your Needs
- Does the information relate to your topic or answer your question?
- Who is the intended audience?
- Is the information at an appropriate level (not too elementary or advanced)?
- Have you looked at a variety of sources before determining this is one you will use?

**Questions to Ask:**
- Does this directly address my research question?
- Is this the type of source I need (academic, professional, general)?
- Is the information too basic or too advanced for my needs?
- Would I be comfortable citing this source?

### 3. Authority: Source of the Information
- Who is the author/publisher/source/sponsor?
- What are the author's credentials or organizational affiliations?
- Is the author qualified to write on this topic?
- Is there contact information, such as a publisher or email address?
- Does the URL reveal anything about the author or source?

**Questions to Ask:**
- Who wrote/published this?
- What are their credentials/expertise?
- Are they affiliated with a reputable organization?
- Is there evidence of bias or conflict of interest?

### 4. Accuracy: Reliability and Truthfulness
- Where does the information come from?
- Is the information supported by evidence?
- Has the information been reviewed or refereed?
- Can you verify any of the information in another source?
- Does the language or tone seem unbiased and free of emotion?
- Are there spelling, grammar, or typographical errors?

**Questions to Ask:**
- Is the information supported by evidence?
- Can facts be verified elsewhere?
- Has it been peer-reviewed or edited?
- Is the writing professional and error-free?
- Do other sources confirm this information?

### 5. Purpose: Reason the Information Exists
- What is the purpose of the information? To inform, teach, sell, entertain, or persuade?
- Do the authors/sponsors make their intentions or purpose clear?
- Is the information fact, opinion, or propaganda?
- Does the point of view appear objective and impartial?
- Are there political, ideological, cultural, religious, institutional, or personal biases?

**Questions to Ask:**
- Why was this created?
- Is it trying to inform, persuade, sell, or entertain?
- Is there obvious bias?
- Are alternative viewpoints presented fairly?

## Domain-Specific Evaluation Guidelines

### Academic Sources
**High Quality Indicators:**
- Published in peer-reviewed journals
- Authors with relevant academic credentials
- Clear methodology section
- References to other scholarly work
- Published by reputable academic publishers

**Red Flags:**
- No peer-review process mentioned
- Published in predatory journals
- Authors without relevant expertise
- Poorly described methodology
- No citations or references

### News and Media Sources
**High Quality Indicators:**
- Clear distinction between news and opinion
- Corrections policy published
- Multiple sources cited
- Transparent about funding/sponsorship
- Professional editorial standards

**Red Flags:**
- Sensational headlines
- Anonymous sources without justification
- No corrections policy
- Excessive ads or sponsored content
- Mixing opinion with news reporting

### Government and Organizational Sources
**High Quality Indicators:**
- Official .gov, .edu, or .org domain
- Clear organizational structure
- Contact information provided
- Regular updates and maintenance
- Transparency about data collection methods

**Red Flags:**
- Outdated information
- Broken links
- No clear authorship
- Political bias in presentation
- Contradicts other official sources

### Commercial and Corporate Sources
**High Quality Indicators:**
- Clear about commercial intent
- Accurate product/service information
- Contact and support information
- Professional presentation
- Compliance with regulations

**Red Flags:**
- Hidden costs or conditions
- Exaggerated claims
- Poor customer reviews elsewhere
- Lack of transparency
- Aggressive marketing tactics

## Fact-Checking Techniques

### 1. Lateral Reading
Instead of staying on one site, open multiple tabs to:
- Check author credentials on other sites
- Verify claims against other sources
- Look for consensus among experts
- Check if site has reputation for accuracy

### 2. Reverse Image Search
Use tools like Google Reverse Image Search or TinEye to:
- Verify authenticity of images
- Find original source of images
- Check if images are being used out of context
- Detect manipulated or AI-generated images

### 3. Claim Verification Process
**Step 1: Identify the Claim**
- What exactly is being claimed?
- Who is making the claim?
- What evidence is provided?

**Step 2: Find Original Source**
- Trace claims back to original research/report
- Check if sources are being cited correctly
- Look for primary sources rather than summaries

**Step 3: Check Multiple Sources**
- Use at least 3 independent reliable sources
- Look for consensus among experts
- Note any disagreements and investigate why

**Step 4: Evaluate Evidence**
- Is evidence from reputable sources?
- Is it current and relevant?
- Is there enough evidence to support the claim?
- Are there alternative explanations?

### 4. Expert Consensus Checking
- Search for systematic reviews or meta-analyses
- Check professional organization statements
- Look for surveys of expert opinion
- Review conference proceedings and position papers

## Bias Detection

### Types of Bias to Watch For
1. **Confirmation Bias**: Presenting only information that supports a position
2. **Selection Bias**: Using unrepresentative samples or examples
3. **Funding Bias**: Research influenced by funding sources
4. **Publication Bias**: Only publishing results that show significant findings
5. **Cultural Bias**: Assuming one cultural perspective is universal

### Questions to Detect Bias
- What perspectives are missing?
- Who benefits from this information?
- What alternative explanations exist?
- Are counterarguments presented fairly?
- Is language emotionally charged or neutral?

## Digital Literacy Skills

### 1. URL Analysis
- Check domain extensions (.gov, .edu, .org, .com)
- Look for HTTPS security
- Analyze subdomains and path structure
- Check for URL shortening services

### 2. About Page Review
- Always check "About Us" or "Contact" pages
- Look for mission statements and values
- Check organizational structure
- Review editorial policies

### 3. Author Investigation
- Search author name with other keywords
- Check academic profiles (Google Scholar, ResearchGate)
- Look for other publications by same author
- Verify institutional affiliations

### 4. Date Verification
- Check multiple dates (published, updated, reviewed)
- Look for "last updated" timestamps
- Verify if information is still current
- Check for more recent developments

## Common Misinformation Patterns

### 1. Out-of-Context Information
- Statistics without proper context
- Quotes taken out of context
- Images used misleadingly
- Partial information presented as complete

### 2. False Equivalence
- Treating unequal things as equal
- False balance in reporting
- Equating opinion with fact
- Misrepresenting scientific consensus

### 3. Emotional Manipulation
- Using fear, anger, or outrage
- Creating us-vs-them narratives
- Appealing to identity or values
- Using anecdotal evidence over data

### 4. Technical Deception
- Manipulated images or videos
- Fake websites mimicking real ones
- AI-generated content
- Deepfakes and synthetic media

## Quick Evaluation Checklist

### Before Trusting Information
- [ ] Source identified and credible
- [ ] Author qualified and unbiased
- [ ] Information current and relevant
- [ ] Evidence provided and verifiable
- [ ] Multiple sources confirm
- [ ] Purpose clear and appropriate
- [ ] No red flags detected

### When in Doubt
1. Don't share immediately
2. Verify with multiple sources
3. Check with fact-checking organizations
4. Consider potential biases
5. Wait for more information if needed

Remember: It's better to be slow and accurate than fast and wrong. Good information literacy protects both you and others from misinformation.
